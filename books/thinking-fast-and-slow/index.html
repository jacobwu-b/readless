Thinking, Fast and Slow

Daniel Kahneman, 2011
Decision-making Cognitive bias Behavioral economics Rationality
Core Thesis

Human thought runs on two systems. System 1 is automatic, fast, and emotional — it makes most of your decisions without telling you. System 2 is deliberate and logical — but lazy, and mostly narrates justifications for what System 1 already decided. Most human error is not stupidity; it is the predictable misfiring of a brain built for a different world.

Key Insights
  • Loss aversion is ~2× more powerful than equivalent gain
    • Losing $100 feels roughly twice as painful as gaining $100 feels good
    • Drives: holding losing investments too long, avoiding career risk, staying in bad situations
    • We evaluate outcomes relative to a shifting reference point — not in absolute terms
    • Reframing a loss as a "foregone gain" measurably changes willingness to act
  • Cognitive ease is the brain's silent "true" signal — and it lies
    • Fluent, familiar, repeated information is flagged as credible regardless of accuracy
    • Bold fonts, clear printing, and rhyming statements all increase perceived truth
    • The things that feel obviously right deserve the most scrutiny, not the least
  • Anchoring distorts all estimation — including by experts
    • The first number encountered pulls all subsequent estimates toward it, even when arbitrary
    • In one study, judges given a randomly rolled number handed out measurably different sentences
    • Awareness barely helps — the only defense is forming your own estimate before seeing theirs
  • The planning fallacy is immune to experience
    • Experts consistently underestimate time, cost, and risk — even with long track records of being wrong
    • Inside view: focus on this plan's specifics → optimistic by default
    • Outside view: what happened to comparable efforts? → almost always more accurate
  • We have two selves with completely different agendas
    • Experiencing self: lives moment to moment
    • Remembering self: edits the past — weighted heavily by peak and ending, not duration
    • The remembering self makes your decisions; the experiencing self lives with them
    • Implications for vacations, careers, relationships — they're often optimized for the wrong self

"Nothing in life is as important as you think it is while you are thinking about it."

Watch Out For
  • Substitution
    • When a hard question is asked, System 1 silently answers an easier one instead
    • System 2 rarely notices the swap — and endorses the answer anyway
  • The halo effect
    • One positive trait (confidence, attractiveness) contaminates all subsequent judgments
    • First impressions are far stickier and more consequential than they should be
  • Narrative fallacy
    • We compulsively construct causal stories from random events
    • Then mistake the story for an explanation — and feel certain about it

Apply This
  • Run a pre-mortem before any major commitment
    • Imagine it's one year later and the project has failed — write down exactly why
    • Forces System 2 engagement before the decision is locked in
  • Anchor first in any negotiation
    • Whoever names a number first controls the frame — use this deliberately
    • When receiving an anchor: form your own estimate before engaging with theirs
  • Seek the outside view on your plans
    • Find the reference class — what happened to comparable efforts historically?
    • That base rate beats your optimistic inside view almost every time
  • Slow down when a decision feels obviously right
    • Cognitive ease is a yellow flag — comfort means System 1 is running unopposed
    • High-stakes + frictionless feeling = time to pause and engage System 2
Reflection Questions
1
Think of a significant decision you made recently that felt obviously right. Which system was actually driving it — and how would you ever know for certain?
2
Where in your life does loss aversion keep you from a bet with clearly positive expected value? What changes if you reframe it purely as a gain?
3
Your remembering self makes your decisions; your experiencing self lives with them. Are they currently optimizing for the same thing?
4
When you plan a project, do you consult the base rate — what happened to comparable efforts? If not, what's actually driving your confidence?
